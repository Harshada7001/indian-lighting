 Abstract Millions of people around the world suffer from mental disorders that significantly affect their thoughts and behaviors. Detecting these conditions in a timely manner is both challenging and vital, as early intervention can offer critical support before the illness worsens. A promising strategy for early detection involves analyzing how individuals express themselves, particularly through writing and social media activity. This article explores two computational models developed to identify the presence and changes in emotions conveyed by social media users.

 The analysis is based on two recent public datasets related to depression and anorexia. The results demonstrate that the proposed emotional representations effectively capture key indicators of these mental health conditions. Furthermore, combining both models improves performance—matching the best-reported method for detecting depression and coming within 1% of the top-performing approach for anorexia. In addition, these models show potential for enhancing the interpretability of the results.

LITERATURE SURVEY Depression is a multifaceted mental health condition that extends far beyond feelings of sadness [1].

 It manifests as a persistent lack of interest in previously enjoyable activities, coupled with a profound sense of hopelessness and despair. This condition disrupts not only how individuals feel but also how they think and act, often leading to significant challenges in daily life. Research into the automated detection of depression has primarily leveraged data collected through crowdsourcing, targeting individuals who have reported receiving treatment for clinical depression. One widely used method involves analyzing words and word n-grams as features in traditional classification algorithms.

 The objective is to identify patterns in the language of individuals with depression by comparing their vocabulary to that of healthy users. However, this approach faces limitations, particularly due to the substantial overlap in the vocabulary used by both groups, making precise differentiation challenging. Another approach involves the use of the Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) framework [2], which classifies text into psychologically meaningful categories such as social connections, cognitive styles, and individual differences.

 While this method has enhanced understanding of mental health through psychological profiling, its ability to outperform basic word-based analysis remains marginal. Recent advancements have combined LIWC-based features with deep learning models, such as LSTM and CNN, as part of ensemble techniques. These integrations have significantly improved detection accuracy, demonstrating the potential of hybrid approaches in identifying depression. In addition to linguistic analysis, researchers are exploring natural language processing (NLP) techniques to analyze content like suicide notes.

 Studies comparing the classification accuracy of NLP algorithms with mental health professionals have highlighted NLP’s potential in identifying individuals at risk. Automated systems using social media data are also emerging as effective tools for detecting mental health conditions. Platforms like Twitter and Facebook serve as rich data sources, offering insights into linguistic patterns, emotional cues, and behavioral trends indicative of depression or other disorders.

 These systems, capable of large-scale monitoring, can provide real-time assessments of users' mental states, enabling early intervention and complementing traditional diagnostic methods. Subtle changes in language tone, increased negativity, or reduced social engagement often serve as red flags for emerging mental health issues. Integrating such technologies into existing healthcare frameworks holds promise for more comprehensive mental health support. However, the ethical implications of using personal data for mental health monitoring must be addressed.

 Issues like consent, data security, and the risk of misuse must be carefully managed to ensure user privacy. Balancing the benefits of early detection with the need to protect individual rights will be critical as these technologies evolve.  Detecting these issues early is challenging but essential, as it opens the opportunity to provide help before the condition worsens [4]. This approach involves identifying two computational representations designed to capture both the presence and the evolution of emotions expressed by social media users.  One widely used method involves static and dynamic representations, both referred to as BoSE, which are based on two key hypotheses.  To address this, we propose representing each user with a histogram of sub-emotions, which are identified by clustering the word embeddings within broad emotional categories. The second hypothesis is that individuals with depression and anorexia tend to display greater emotional variability than healthy individuals. In this case, we represent each user with a set of statistical values that describe how the frequency of these sub-emotions changes over time. The results suggest that the presence and variability of emotions captured by these representations provide valuable insights into social media users suffering from depression or anorexia.

LOGISTIC REGRESSION CLASSIFIERS Logistic regression is a statistical technique that investigates the association between a categorical outcome variable and a set of predictor variables. In binary logistic regression, the outcome has two possible states (e.g., success/failure, presence/absence). Categorical outcomes with more than two levels, for instance, marital status, necessitate the use of multinomial logistic regression.

 While distinct from multiple regression in its focus on categorical outcomes, logistic regression shares a similar analytical framework. It is frequently compared to discriminant analysis, another method for analyzing categorical responses. However, logistic regression enjoys greater flexibility due to its relaxed assumptions. Unlike discriminant analysis, it does not require normally distributed predictor variables, expanding its applicability across various datasets.

 This methodology encompasses both binary and multinomial logistic regression, accommodating both numerical and categorical predictors. The output typically includes the regression equation, measures of model fit, odds ratios quantifying the effect of predictors on the outcome, confidence intervals for these estimates, likelihood values, and deviance statistics.
